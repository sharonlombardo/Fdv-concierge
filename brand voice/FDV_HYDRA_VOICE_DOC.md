## THE ISLAND IN A SENTENCE

Hydra is what Mykonos was before the money ruined it — car-free, artist-haunted, and quietly glamorous. Two hours by ferry from Athens, it's a bare rock of an island where Leonard Cohen wrote his best songs, donkeys carry your luggage, and the most dramatic thing you'll see all day is the light changing on stone.

---

## WHERE TO STAY

**Orloff Boutique Hotel** — Built in 1796, this former convent is now an 8-room boutique hotel that feels like staying in someone's beautifully maintained private house. Calm neighborhood, 100 meters from the port. The courtyard is the kind of place where you read for three hours without noticing. Classic elegance with a bohemian edge — very Hydra.

**Bratsera Hotel** — A converted 19th-century sponge factory. Stone walls, exposed beams, nautical touches that feel earned rather than decorative. The courtyard pool is a genuine luxury on an island without resort infrastructure. Close to the port but set back enough to feel quiet. The on-site restaurant is one of the better meals on the island.

**Cotommatae 1810** — A restored sea captain's mansion with the kind of deep-walled, cool rooms that feel like they've been here for centuries, because they have. The garden courtyard is worth the stay alone. Great for couples who want something intimate without fuss.

**Hydrea** — Set above the port in a mansion from 1803. Every suite has views of the Saronic Gulf. Repeatedly named Greece's Best Historic Hotel. If you want a small property with real grandeur — sweeping views, stone archways, the whole drama of Hydra beneath you — this is it.

**For something different: Mandraki Beach Resort** — The only proper beachfront option. A 25-minute walk from the port (or a quick water taxi). Only seven suites plus a villa. The restaurant is exceptional. This is for someone who wants to swim and eat and not think about anything else.

## WHERE TO EAT

**Omilos** — The restaurant everyone talks about, and for once they're right. Perched above the sea among pine trees, it started in the '60s as a legendary cosmopolitan hangout. The setting is genuinely stunning — sunset here is non-negotiable. Elevated Greek cuisine. Book ahead.

**Téchnē** — All-day restaurant in a lush courtyard. Greek and Mediterranean dishes with serious attention. This is where you go when you want a beautiful room and food that matches it. Some vegan options, which is rare on the island.

**Douskos (Xeri Elia)** — Leonard Cohen's taverna. He was photographed playing guitar under the pine tree out front in 1960, and the family has run it for 200 years. The food is traditional, generous, unfussy. Go for the atmosphere and history as much as the meal. Get a table under the tree if you can.

**Pirofani** — The hidden gem in Kamini. White-washed courtyard, blue chairs, family-run since 1994. The almond-crusted sea bass is the kind of dish people tell their friends about. This is where you go when you want to eat like a local, not like a tourist.

**Sunset Restaurant** — One of the most celebrated views on the island — perched on the cliffs below the Kountouriotis mansion. Gourmet Greek cuisine, and they earn the name. Go for dinner, stay for the light.

**Caprice** — In a converted sponge warehouse behind the port. The pizza has handmade thin dough and seasonal ingredients. The cuttlefish risotto is excellent. This is your second-night dinner — the place that feels like yours.

**Isalos** — Right on the harbor, one of the first spots you see getting off the ferry. Open year-round, which matters. Fresh salads, good sandwiches, a reassuring presence. Great for a casual first or last meal.

**The Pirate Bar** — A Hydra institution for 40+ years, right on the harbor next to the Kountouriotis statue. This is where everyone ends up eventually — shipowners' kids, models, sailors, artists, and you. Good music, relaxed energy that shifts to dancing as the night goes on. It's an all-day spot now (breakfast, coffee, light lunch) but the evening is when it comes alive. Hydra's living room.

## WHAT NOT TO MISS

**The DESTE Foundation Slaughterhouse** — A former abattoir turned summer exhibition space by collector Dakis Joannou. Past installations include Jeff Koons, Urs Fischer, Kiki Smith. Raw, atmospheric, and serious. Open summer only. This is the reason the international art world pays attention to a Greek island with a population of 2,000.

**Leonard Cohen's house** — Still privately owned by his family, so be respectful. The street was named after him in 2017. You won't find it easily — there are no real street signs on Hydra. Ask a local. The Leonard Cohen memorial bench overlooking the sea is the better place to sit and take it in.

**Swimming at Spilia and Hydronetta** — Forget sandy beaches. You swim off the rocks here, into water so clear it barely looks real. Hydronetta has a bar built into the cliffs. Spilia is more local, more raw. Both are walking distance from town.

**The walk to Kamini** — A 20-minute cliffside walk from town that passes the Cohen memorial bench. End at Pirofani for lunch. This is the best thing you can do on your first afternoon.

**The harbor at night** — After the day-trippers leave on the evening ferry, Hydra becomes a different island. Lanterns come on. The stone paths are almost empty. The harbor reflects the lights. This is what you came for.

## WHAT TO SKIP

**Day-tripping from Athens.** Everyone says you can do Hydra as a day trip. You can. But you'd be experiencing the island at its worst — crowded harbor, donkey traffic jams, the surface version. Stay at least two nights. The real Hydra starts at 7 PM when the ferries leave.

**Mykonos comparisons.** People will tell you Hydra is "like Mykonos but quieter." It's not. It's a fundamentally different proposition — no beach clubs, no DJs, no velvet ropes. If you want that, go to Mykonos. If you want to read a book and eat well and think about nothing, come here.

## THE VIBE

Hydra is the island that proves you don't need much. No cars. No motorcycles. No new construction — the entire island is a protected architectural monument. Donkeys carry your bags. You walk everywhere. The pace isn't slow because someone decided it should be — it's slow because the infrastructure won't allow anything else.

The light here is the real attraction. It hits the white stone houses in the morning and turns the whole harbor into something between a painting and a photograph. By evening it goes gold, then deep blue, and the water in the harbor reflects all of it back.

There's an artistic seriousness to Hydra that feels genuine, not performed. Cohen lived here. Joni Mitchell lived in a cave nearby. David Gilmour and Polly Samson have a house here. Dakis Joannou shows Jeff Koons in a former slaughterhouse. But nobody's making a scene about it. The art world presence is quiet, confident, and woven into daily life.

This is the Greek island for people who don't need a scene — who'd rather sit at a harbor taverna with a glass of wine and watch the cats negotiate with the fishermen.

## GETTING DRESSED

Hydra is the most effortlessly stylish of the Greek islands because nobody's trying. The dress code is: linen, flat shoes, something that looks good with a tan and wet hair.

Cobblestones and steep stone steps are the reality. Leave the heels at home — not as a fashion statement but because you'll literally fall. Flat leather sandals, espadrilles, or simple slides. The A Emery Kir Sandal was basically designed for this island.

During the day: cotton dresses, loose pants, a white linen shirt that's lived in. Nothing precious. You'll be climbing hills, swimming off rocks, walking to lunch with salt in your hair. Pack things you don't mind getting slightly wrecked.

Evening is easy here — it's not formal, it's just considered. A simple dress, good earrings, bare legs. The restaurants that matter (Omilos, Sunset) have atmosphere enough. You don't need to match it — just don't fight it.

Color: black, white, cream, navy, sand. Black especially — it's moody, it photographs beautifully against white stone, and it's the FDV signature for a reason. The island is a palette already — white stone, blue water, green pine, terracotta roof. You're part of it whether you try or not.

One coverup or wrap for the wind. Hydra gets a breeze off the Saronic Gulf in the evening that you'll be glad to have something for.

## PRACTICAL

**Getting there:** Ferry from Piraeus (Athens) — roughly 2 hours on the high-speed hydrofoil, or 3.5 hours on the slower conventional ferry. The hydrofoil is worth it.

**No cars.** Really, none. No motorcycles, no bicycles. Donkeys carry luggage. Water taxis connect the harbor to beaches and the Mandraki area. You walk everywhere else. This is not quaint — it's the entire point.

**Timing:** May-June is ideal — warm, not crowded, galleries starting to open. July-August is the social season but gets hot and busy, especially weekends when Athenians come over. September is the sweet spot — warm sea, golden light, fewer people. Most seasonal places close by late October.

**Money:** Many places are cash-only. ATMs exist but are limited. Bring cash.

**The one thing nobody tells you:** Hydra's harbor is essentially a theater. The horseshoe shape means everyone can see everyone. Where you sit, when you appear, what you're wearing — it's all part of a daily performance that locals have been watching for centuries. Don't take it too seriously. But know that it's happening.
