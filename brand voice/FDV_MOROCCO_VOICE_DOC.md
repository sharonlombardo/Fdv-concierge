## WHERE TO STAY

**El Fenn** is my go-to for at least the first few nights. It's beautiful, obviously, but what makes it practical is the restaurant, the rooftop, and the gift shop all in one place. If you're there for business — and a lot of designers are — that matters. The location is the real win though. You're right on the edge of the medina, close to the street where you can grab a taxi without walking deep into the narrow streets. This matters a lot at night and if you're solo. GPS does not work well inside the medina. Being on the edge is a genuine advantage, not just a convenience.

**Other riads I love:** Kitu La Marrakech, Riad Jardin Secret, Riad Tarabel. Each has its own personality but they're all the kind of place where you feel held, not just housed.

**For a hotel experience:** La Mamounia. It's glamorous and over the top and fun. A completely different energy from the riads — more spectacle, less intimacy. Both are worth experiencing.

**On guides:** If you stay at any riad, they can usually connect you with a local guide they trust and work with regularly. Take them up on this. A good guide changes everything about how you experience the medina.

## DAY TRIPS & EXPERIENCES

**Essaouira** is a must. It's the surfey, beachy, low-key version of Morocco. A totally different energy from Marrakech — windswept and relaxed. Perfect day trip or overnight.

**La Pause** for a desert night. It's where we did our first FIL DE VIE shoot back in the day. The desert outside Marrakech is stunning and La Pause does it without making it feel like a tourist production.

**Hammam experience** — don't skip this. It's one of those things you can't replicate anywhere else.

**The souks** — obviously. But don't rush through them trying to see everything. Let yourself get a little lost.

**Mustapha Blaoui** for home goods. You could literally walk around in there for hours. Moroccan pottery is incredible — I have a lot of it in our beach house in East Hampton.

---

## THE BIGGEST MISTAKE

Trying to see too many tourist sites. Marrakech is a city you feel, not a city you check off. The magic is in being there — walking, soaking it up, sitting on a rooftop watching the light change. Slow down. You'll see more that way.

---

## DON'T MISS

Rooftop sunset aperitifs. Every single evening if you can. The light in Marrakech at golden hour is unlike anywhere else.

## GETTING DRESSED

**The philosophy:** Linen is your best friend. No synthetic fibers — depending on when you go, it's hot to hotter. Flat sandals. Loose blouses. The FIL DE VIE Marrakech Pants were literally designed for this city.

**On modesty:** Morocco is a modest country. You don't have to literally cover up completely, but you'll feel better and more comfortable in a maxi sundress or pants and a top. Bring a linen wrap for your shoulders — you'll know when you want it.

**On color:** I personally think neutrals work beautifully because there's so much color in the environment — the tiles, the textiles, the spices — you don't need to compete with it. Let the city be the color. You be the canvas.

**The vibe:** Marrakech screams bohemian in the best possible way. This is the time to let yourself go. Get out of tight jeans and too-tailored looks. Let things drape. Let things move.

**Evening:** The restaurants I love are a little bit fabulous, so you'll want to dress up — but not seriously. Think effort without formality. A dress that moves. Good earrings. Done.

**Beauty:** I love a charcoal eye or dark red lipstick in Marrakech. It reminds me of Berber women and it just feels right at home there. Also — Marrakech is a great place to buy scent. I love orange blossom and always buy some when I'm there.

**Photos:** Everyone takes a million photos in Marrakech — it's impossibly photogenic. So yes, you'll want to look cute. But "cute" here means relaxed and intentional, not overdone.

## PRACTICAL NOTES

**Alcohol:** Morocco is largely a dry country. If you like to drink — and I do — you need to make sure the restaurants you choose serve alcohol. All of our recommendations in the guide do.

**Taxis & navigation:** GPS doesn't work well in the medina. Staying near the edge (like El Fenn) means you're close to a main street where taxis can actually reach you. This matters more than people realize, especially at night.

**Timing:** The city has a rhythm. Mornings are for exploring. Afternoons get hot — retreat to your riad, read, rest. Evenings come alive. Don't fight it. Move with it.
