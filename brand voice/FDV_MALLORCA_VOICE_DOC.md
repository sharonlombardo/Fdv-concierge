## THE ISLAND IN A SENTENCE

Mallorca is the Mediterranean island that actually lives up to the fantasy — golden stone villages clinging to mountains above turquoise calas, ancient olive groves, Michelin-starred restaurants in converted farmhouses, and a creative scene centered on Deià that's been drawing artists, writers, and the quietly wealthy for decades. It's Spain's Balearic answer to the Amalfi Coast, but less performative and better dressed.

## WHERE TO STAY

**Belmond La Residencia, Deià** — The most iconic hotel on the island, and possibly in Spain. Two converted stone manor houses perched above the village with terraced gardens, olive trees, and views of the Tramuntana Mountains meeting the sea. El Olivo restaurant is the best meal on the island (book months ahead). This is where you stay if Mallorca is the trip, not a stop.

**Hotel Corazón, between Deià and Sóller** — The island's buzziest new property. Created by photographer Kate Bellm and artist Edgar Lopez, it's 15 rooms of 1970s-inspired bohemia — shaggy carpets, egg-shaped dome showers, no TVs. Sound baths, artist workshops, guided hikes. This is for the woman who wants to be in the mountains, not on a scene. Vogue-recommended and genuinely interesting.

**Finca Serena, countryside** — 40 hectares of olive groves, vineyards, and lavender. 25 minimalist rooms in honey-stone buildings. The spa is in a converted cowshed. The restaurant uses the kitchen garden. This is the deep exhale — when you want to disappear into the landscape and not come up for days.

**Sant Francesc Hotel Singular, Palma** — A 19th-century neoclassical mansion in the old town. Contemporary design, original frescoes, locally produced ceramics. The rooftop pool with city views is where you want to be at 6 PM with something cold. Best Palma base for someone who wants design and walkability.

**Cap Rocat, near Palma** — A converted military fortress on its own private bay. Dramatic, stone, elemental. The restaurant serves modern Mallorcan cuisine in a setting that feels like eating inside history. This is the grand gesture hotel — not cozy, but unforgettable.

**Can Ferrereta, Santanyí** — From the same team behind Sant Francesc in Palma. A 17th-century mansion in a charming southern market town. Chef Carlos García at Ocre restaurant spotlights Mediterranean ingredients with a local focus. The southeast coast has a different light — warmer, longer, more golden — and this is the best place to stay in it.

## WHERE TO EAT

**El Olivo, Belmond La Residencia, Deià** — The restaurant that people plan Mallorca trips around. Outdoor terrace overlooking the Deià rooftops with mountains behind and sea beyond. Mediterranean cuisine that's creative without losing its roots. Book for sunset. Book early. This is the dinner.

**Ca's Patró March, Cala Deià** — Built into the rocks above a crystal-clear cove, this is where you eat grilled fish with your feet practically in the Mediterranean. Featured in The Night Manager. The food is simple and perfect because it doesn't need to compete with the view. Book ahead. Go for lunch, swim after.

**Bens d'Avall, between Deià and Sóller** — Michelin-starred, cliffside, jaw-dropping. Perched on the edge of the Tramuntana with the sea below. Locals use it for special occasions. Ask for a table facing the view. Arrive before sunset. Order the tasting menu and trust the chef.

**DINS Santi Taura, Palma** — Michelin-starred Mallorcan cuisine in a room near the cathedral. Chef Santi Taura cooks like a grandmother with a PhD — traditional flavors with modern precision. You can sit at the bar and watch it being made. This is where you understand what Mallorcan food actually is.

**Botanic, Palma** — Condé Nast Traveler named it one of the 40 most beautiful restaurants in the world. Vegetables-first, seasonal, original. A different kind of fine dining — less formal, more thoughtful. The Iberian pork taco alone is worth the visit.

**El Camino, Santa Catalina, Palma** — The longest bar you've ever seen, in the neighborhood that's become Palma's most interesting dining district. Modern tapas, Eddie Hart's team. This is your first-night dinner — the place that tells you Palma is more than a cruise port.

**Xelini, Deià** — The tapas bar that locals actually go to. Informal, lovely garden space. This is where you eat when El Olivo is too much of a production and you just want to sit outside with something simple and good.

**Restaurante Sebastian, Deià** — Beautiful setting, brilliant chef. The kind of place that's been there long enough to be trusted but never got lazy. Recommended by Belmond's own resident curator, which tells you everything.

**Ca Na Toneta, Caimari** — The Solivellas sisters — Maria and Teresa — have spent years rediscovering local producers and breathing new life into traditional Mallorcan recipes. This isn't a restaurant. It's a lifetime project. You'll eat things here that taste like they have a history.

**Ca'n Paco, Banyalbufar** — A glass of local malvasia wine on this terrace at sunset, overlooking the ancient terraced vineyards and the sea. That's it. That's the experience. You don't need more.

**Mercat de l'Olivar, Palma** — The central market. Open mornings until mid-afternoon. The kiosks serve fresh seafood, local charcuterie, and the kind of casual lunch you'd never find in a restaurant. Go hungry.

## WHAT NOT TO MISS

**Deià** — A hillside village of stone cottages, olive trees, and creative energy. Robert Graves lived here for decades. The village is small enough to walk in 20 minutes but rich enough to hold you for days. The walk down to Cala Deià is essential — pebble beach, transparent water, Ca's Patró March for lunch.

**Valldemossa** — Mountain village famous for the monastery where Chopin and George Sand spent a winter. Stone houses, valley views, artisan shops, quiet cafés. Go in the morning before the day-trippers arrive. Leave by lunch.

**The Tramuntana drive** — The coastal road along the entire northwest coast. Dramatic cliffs, hidden villages (Estellencs, Banyalbufar), terraced vineyards from the Moorish era. This is the drive you do on day two — windows down, nowhere to be.

**Sóller and the vintage train** — A scenic mountain railway from Palma to the elegant town of Sóller. Then a tram to Port de Sóller. It's touristy, yes, but it's also genuinely beautiful and surprisingly fun. The kind of thing you'd never plan but always remember.

**Cala swimming** — The calas (small coves) are Mallorca's secret weapon. Cala Deià for drama, Cala Llombards for Instagram-perfect turquoise, Es Trenc for the closest thing to Caribbean sand. **Cala s'Almunia** for the early morning first-swim-of-the-day that multiple locals called the best feeling on the island — turquoise water, surrounding cliffs, go before anyone else arrives. Go early to any of them — by noon they're packed.

**Cap de Formentor at sunset** — Every insider we spoke to said this independently. The drive to the northern tip of the island, the cliffs, the light. Go for sunset. Don't overthink it.

**Cabrera island** — A small, protected island off the south coast. A pristine sanctuary — the escape from the escape. When Mallorca itself feels too busy (July, August), this is where the locals disappear to.

**Arquinesia Perfumes, Palma** — Artisanal scents inspired by the Mediterranean and the Balearic Islands. Probably the most beautiful shop in Palma. If you love fragrance, you'll lose an hour here easily.

**Datura Studio Isla, Deià** — A New York brand's Mallorcan outpost. Sustainable, high-end natural fabrics — garments, shoes, sunglasses, bags. Beautifully curated. The kind of shop where you buy the one thing you'll wear for years.

## WHAT TO SKIP

**The mega beach clubs on the south coast.** Nikki Beach and its ilk are Mallorca trying to be Ibiza. That's not why you came. If you want a beach club, Gran Folies is the one that doesn't feel embarrassing.

**Magaluf.** Obviously. But also the entire strip of mass-tourism resorts on the southwest. A different island from the one we're talking about. Pretend it doesn't exist.

**Trying to see everything in 3 days.** Mallorca is not a checklist. Pick Palma or the Tramuntana coast and commit. The island rewards depth, not breadth. A week in Deià with one day trip to Palma is better than racing between five towns.

## THE VIBE

Mallorca is bigger and more varied than people expect. There are effectively three islands sharing one coastline — the sophisticated city of Palma, the bohemian mountain villages of the Tramuntana, and the beach resorts of the south and east (which we're ignoring). The FDV Mallorca is the first two.

Palma has the energy of a small European capital. Cathedral, old town, excellent restaurants, a growing design scene. It's walkable, cultured, and surprisingly cosmopolitan. Think Barcelona's little sister who reads more.

The Tramuntana coast is the soul. Deià, Valldemossa, Sóller, Fornalutx — stone villages in mountain folds, olive terraces, the sea always just below. The light is golden in a way that Italy wishes it could copyright. Robert Graves chose Deià over everywhere else in the world and never left. That tells you everything.

The pace is slower than Hydra (which already felt slow) but more social. Mallorca likes a long lunch. It likes an aperitif. It likes to sit and watch the light change on stone. There's no rush because there's nowhere better to be.

## GETTING DRESSED

Mallorca is warmer and more relaxed than Morocco, but with a similar "dress for the room" sensibility at dinner.

**Daytime:** You'll live in swimwear and coverups. Linen everything. The FIL DE VIE caftans were made for this island — the Bella Mini for the beach, the Lillith for a village walk. Flat sandals or espadrilles. A good straw bag.

**The beach-to-village transition:** This is where Mallorca style happens. You're swimming at Cala Deià at noon and walking into the village for lunch at 2. A linen wrap dress over a bikini, good sunglasses, sandals that can handle cobblestones. Nothing precious.

**Evening:** The restaurants that matter (El Olivo, Bens d'Avall, DINS) have atmosphere but not dress codes. A summer dress that moves, good earrings, bare shoulders. Mallorca evening is more relaxed than Morocco evening — less dramatic, more golden.

**Color:** The island's palette is warm — golden stone, terracotta, olive green, sea blue. Whites and creams work beautifully. But black — always black. Against the golden stone it's striking, and it's the FDV signature for a reason.

**Footwear reality:** Cobblestones everywhere. The walk down to Cala Deià is steep and rocky. Flat sandals, espadrilles, or something you can actually walk in. Save anything with a heel for Palma, and even then, only dinner.

**Shopping note:** Mallorca has surprising shopping. Datura Studio Isla in Deià for sustainable fashion. Arquinesia for scent. The shoe shops in Palma are genuinely good — Spanish leather, handmade, well-priced. Leave room in the suitcase.

## PRACTICAL

**Getting there:** Direct flights from most European cities to Palma airport. From the US, connect through Madrid, Barcelona, or London. Airport to Palma old town is 15 minutes. Airport to Deià is about 45 minutes.

**Car:** You need one. Unlike Hydra or central Marrakech, Mallorca is an island you drive. The Tramuntana road is winding but stunning. Rent from the airport, return to the airport. International license not required for US travelers in Spain.

**Timing:** May-June and September-October are ideal. July-August is hot and crowded, especially weekends when half of Madrid flies in. Spring has wildflowers. September has the warmest sea. **The insider's secret:** mid-October through March. Multiple hotel owners said this independently — low season Mallorca is a different island. Mild, quiet, the restaurants still open, the walks are better, and it's yours.

**Money:** Spain is on the euro. Cards accepted almost everywhere. Tipping is appreciated but not expected at the same level as the US — round up or leave 5-10%.

**Language:** Spanish and Catalan (Mallorquín dialect). English widely spoken in tourist areas and good restaurants. A few words of Spanish go a long way.

**The one thing nobody tells you:** The wind. Mallorca gets a strong sea breeze, especially on the Tramuntana coast. It keeps things cool, which is lovely, but it can whip up out of nowhere at a beach cala. Bring a wrap. And book your Cala Deià lunch for a day with a calm forecast — the restaurant closes in bad weather.
