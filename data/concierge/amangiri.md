## THE PLACE IN A SENTENCE

Amangiri is what happens when someone builds a five-star resort and then makes it disappear into 900 acres of ancient desert. No sign of civilization for miles. Just you, the canyons, and a pool wrapped around a 165-million-year-old rock formation. It's the most beautiful place in America that most Americans don't know about.

---

## WHAT IT IS

Amangiri is an Aman resort in Canyon Point, southern Utah — technically just over the Arizona border, near Page. 34 suites in the main resort, plus Camp Sarika with 10 tented pavilions for a more immersive experience. Three MICHELIN Keys. Condé Nast Traveller Gold List 2026. All meals included. The architecture is modernist concrete and sandstone that literally blends into the canyon walls — you can barely see the building from a distance. That's intentional. The landscape is the point.

This isn't a beach trip or a city break. This is a reset. The desert strips everything down — there's nothing to perform for, nothing to keep up with. Just silence, enormous sky, and rock that's been here for millions of years.

---

## WHERE TO STAY

**Main Resort** — The iconic Amangiri you've seen in photos. 34 suites with clean lines, floor-to-ceiling desert views, outdoor lounges, and fire pits. Select suites have sky lofts and private pools. The central pool wraps around a natural sandstone mesa — it's genuinely one of the most photographed hotel pools in the world, and it's better in person. The main pavilion has a wood-burning oven kitchen with picture windows onto the desert.

**Camp Sarika** — A five-minute golf cart ride from the main resort. 10 luxury tented pavilions, each with a private plunge pool, outdoor shower facing your own canyon view, and a fire pit. This is for someone who wants the Aman experience but more raw, more private. You sleep in a tent — a very, very nice tent — and wake up to sandstone and silence. If the main resort is the architectural statement, Camp Sarika is the whispered secret.

**How to choose:** Main resort for first-timers, architecture lovers, and anyone who wants the pool experience. Camp Sarika for returners, couples wanting total privacy, and people who want to feel like they're sleeping in the desert (but with a 500-thread-count safety net).

## WHAT TO DO

**The pool.** Start here. End here. The pool wraps around the mesa in a way that makes you feel like you're swimming inside the landscape. Morning is still and reflective. Afternoon catches the sun on the canyon walls. Evening turns everything gold and then deep blue. You don't need to do anything else, but you will.

**Via Ferrata.** Iron-rung climbing routes bolted into the mesa walls. Sounds terrifying. It's actually exhilarating and suitable for beginners with a guide. You end up on top of the mesa looking out at everything — the desert, the canyons, the resort tiny below you. This is the experience people talk about for years.

**Hiking.** 12+ miles of marked trails on the property alone, plus access to Zion, Bryce Canyon, and Grand Canyon for day trips. The on-property hikes range from gentle to demanding. Go with a guide — they know the geology, the ecology, the Navajo history. It's a different hike when someone can tell you what you're looking at.

**Hot air balloon at dawn.** You lift off as the sun comes up over the mesas. The air is still. The desert is below you. You don't speak. Nobody needs to tell you not to.

**Helicopter to the national parks.** Amangiri will charter a helicopter to Zion, Bryce, or the Grand Canyon. Extravagant, yes. Also: seeing the Colorado Plateau from the air changes how you understand the earth.

**Horseback riding.** Desert trails on horseback, with views that make you understand why people made Westerns here.

**Stargazing.** Zero light pollution. The Milky Way is visible to the naked eye. The resort provides guides and blankets. Go to your suite's fire pit, look up, and remember that you're very small.

## THE SPA

The 25,000-square-foot Aman Spa is not a nice add-on. It's half the reason people come. Five pavilions designed around reflection and natural light. Massages, body wraps, facials, flotation therapy, and a crystal sound bath that skeptics swear by afterward. The reflecting pool, sauna, and steam room are all oriented toward the desert — you're never not looking at landscape. Book a treatment for your first afternoon. It recalibrates you from travel mode to desert mode.

## EATING

All meals are included and served in two locations — the main pavilion restaurant and the Camp Sarika dining area. The cuisine is American Southwest-inspired, made primarily from locally sourced ingredients. Wood-burning oven is the centerpiece. The food is good — not the reason you came, but never a disappointment. Order the Prickly Pear Margarita or the Sage Mojito. They taste like the desert.

The chef's counter lets you watch the kitchen work. The main dining room offers views of either the desert or the pool — request your preference.

**Honest note:** Some guests find the food very good but not extraordinary given the price point. The resort isn't a food destination. It's a landscape destination with very good food in it. If you want a Michelin-starred meal, fly to Palma. If you want to eat something well-prepared while staring at a canyon, you're in the right place.

## WHAT NOT TO MISS

**Sunset from your suite's terrace.** The desert turns every color — orange, pink, deep red, purple — in about 20 minutes. Then it goes dark fast and the stars come out. You won't believe it's real. Do this every night.

**The drive in.** Whether you come from Las Vegas (4.5 hours) or Page (25 minutes), the approach is dramatic. You drive through increasingly empty, increasingly beautiful landscape until you're in the middle of nowhere and then suddenly — there it is, barely visible against the rock. That arrival is part of the experience.

**A morning alone.** Wake up early, before anyone else. Walk one of the shorter trails. The desert at dawn is a different temperature, a different color, a different silence than the rest of the day. This is the moment Amangiri is really about.

---

## WHAT TO SKIP

**Trying to do too much.** Amangiri will offer you a menu of 20+ activities. You don't need to do all of them. The point is stillness, not a checklist. Two or three experiences in a three-night stay is plenty. Leave room for doing nothing.

**Day-tripping to every national park.** Yes, Zion and Bryce and the Grand Canyon are nearby. But if you spend every day in a car driving to parks, you'll miss the property itself — which is the whole point of paying Aman prices. Pick one park excursion. Spend the rest of the time on property.

**Coming in summer.** July and August hit 100°F+. Hiking is miserable. The pool is the only option. November through April is ideal — cool mornings, perfect hiking weather, crisp stargazing nights. November is the sweet spot.

## GETTING DRESSED

This is the most casual destination in the FDV world. The desert doesn't care what you're wearing — and neither does Aman.

**Daytime:** Athletic wear for activities, swimwear for the pool, comfortable layers for hiking. The temperature swings dramatically — mornings can be cool, afternoons warm, evenings cold. Layer.

**The resort uniform:** Soft, neutral, unstructured. Linen pants, a cashmere wrap, bare feet. The color palette here is the desert itself — sand, terracotta, cream, sage. You are literally inside the palette. Wear it.

**Evening:** The dining room is elegant but unstiff. A simple dress or good pants and a silk top. Nothing formal. The most dressed-up person at dinner is still wearing flat sandals.

**Black in the desert:** It works — especially at evening when the fire pits are lit. A black caftan against the sandstone at golden hour is a photograph.

**What to pack that you might forget:** A warm layer for stargazing (it gets cold at night, even in spring). Good walking shoes for trails. Sunscreen — the UV at altitude is intense. A hat.

## PRACTICAL

**Getting there:** Fly into Page Municipal Airport (PGA) — 25 minutes from the resort. Or fly into Las Vegas or Phoenix and drive (4.5 hours through spectacular scenery). St. George, Utah and Flagstaff, Arizona are about 2.5 hours. The resort arranges transfers.

**How long to stay:** Three nights is the consensus. Two isn't enough to decompress. Four starts to feel long unless you're doing serious national park excursions. Three is the sweet spot.

**Cost:** This is a significant splurge. Suites start around $3,000-4,000/night. Camp Sarika pavilions are more. All meals included. Activities are extra. Know that going in. It's worth it — but it's worth it once, done right, not as an annual habit.

**Timing:** November through April is ideal. Cool enough for outdoor adventures, clear skies for stargazing. November is particularly good — few guests, perfect weather. May-June is warm but manageable. July-August is punishing heat. September-October can still be hot.

**Time zone confusion:** The resort is on Arizona time (Mountain Standard), which doesn't observe Daylight Saving. Your phone might disagree. Ask the front desk.

**The one thing nobody tells you:** The silence. Not metaphorical silence — actual silence. No cars, no planes, no ambient city noise. If you've lived in New York for any length of time, the silence at Amangiri will be the first thing you notice and the last thing you forget.
